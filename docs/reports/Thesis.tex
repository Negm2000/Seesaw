The actual weight of the cart seen by the motor is
\begin{equation}
	M_e = M_c + \frac{J_m \cdot K_g^2}{r_{mp}^2}
\end{equation}
Defining the force acting on the cart as
\begin{equation}
	F_c(t) = \alpha \cdot v(t) - B_{emf} \cdot \dot{x}_c(t)
\end{equation}
where
\begin{align*}
	\alpha  & = \frac{k_tK_g\eta_g\eta_m}{r^2_{mp}R_m} &
	B_{emf} & = \frac{k_tk_mK^2_g\eta_g}{r^2_{mp}R_m}
\end{align*}

The kinetic energy takes the form
\begin{equation}
	T = \frac{1}{2}M_e\dot{x}_c^2
\end{equation}
The equation of motion of the system is:
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:cart_eom}
	\begin{aligned}
		                & \frac{\partial^2T}{\partial t \partial \dot{x}_c} - \frac{\partial T}{\partial x_c} & = & -B_{eq,c} \cdot \dot{x}_c + F_c                                     \\
		\Leftrightarrow & M_e \cdot \ddot{x}_c                                                                & = & -B_{eq,c} \cdot \dot{x}_c + \alpha \cdot v - B_{emf}\cdot \dot{x}_c \\
		\Leftrightarrow & M_e \cdot \ddot{x}_c(t) + (B_{eq,c} + B_{emf}) \cdot \dot{x}_c (t)                  & = & \alpha \cdot v(t)
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Define the Lagrangian of the $L = T-V$ where $T$ is the kinetic energy and $V$ the potential energy.
\begin{equation}
	T = \frac{1}{2}M_e \dot{x}_c^2 + \frac{1}{2}(J_{sw} + M_eD_T^2)\dot{\theta}^2 + \frac{1}{2}M_ex_c^2 \dot{\theta}^2 - M_eD_T \dot{x}_c \dot{\theta}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
	V = gM_ex_csin(\theta) + g(M_eD_T + M_{sw}D_C )cos(\theta)
\end{equation}
Using the Euler-Lagrange relation:\\
\begin{equation}
	\left\{
	\begin{aligned}
		\frac{\partial^2L}{\partial t \partial \dot{x}_c} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial x_c}       & = - B_{eq,c} \dot{x}_c + F_c \\
		\frac{\partial^2L}{\partial t \partial \dot{\theta}} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta} & = - B_{sw} \dot{\theta}
	\end{aligned}
	\right.
\end{equation}
The final equations of motion for the complete system are:
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:seesaw_eom}
	\left\{
	\begin{aligned}
		M_e\dot{x}_c - M_eD_T \ddot{\theta} - M_e x_c \dot{\theta}^2 + gM_e sin(\theta)                 & = - B_{eq,c} \dot{x}_c + F_c \\
		M_e \ddot{\theta} x_c^2 + 2M_e \dot{x}_c \dot{\theta} + gM_ecos(\theta)x_c - M_e D_T \ddot{x}_c & +                            \\
		(J_{sw} + M_eD^2_T)\ddot{\theta} - g(M_eD_T - M_{sw}D_C)sin(\theta)                             & = - B_{sw} \dot{\theta}
	\end{aligned}
	\right.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Transfer Function Definition}

Based on the equation of motion described at Eq.~\ref{eq:cart_eom}, we define the transfer function of the system using the Laplace Transform.
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:cart_eom_laplace}
	[M_e \cdot s^2 + (B_{eq,c} + B_{emf}) \cdot s]\cdot X_c(s) = \alpha \cdot V(s)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:cart_tf}
	\begin{aligned}
		G(s) = \frac{X(s)}{V(s)} & = \frac{\alpha}{M_e \cdot s^2 + B_{tot} \cdot s}                           \\
		                         & = \frac{1}{s} \cdot \frac{(\alpha / B_{tot})}{(M_e / B_{tot}) \cdot s + 1}
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Transfer Functions Definition}

Assumptions for linearization :
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Small angles ($\theta \simeq 0$) : $sin(\theta) = \theta$ and $cos(\theta) = 1$
	\item Ignoring high-order velocity terms : $\dot{\theta}^2, x_c^2, \dot{x}_c\dot{\theta} \simeq 0$
\end{enumerate}
After linearizing and replacing $F_c$, the system is:
\begin{equation}
	\left\{
	\begin{aligned}
		M_e\dot{x}_c - M_eD_T \ddot{\theta}
		+ gM_e \theta + B_c \dot{x}_c + B_{emf}\dot{x}_c & =  \alpha V_{DC} \\
		- M_e D_T \ddot{x}_c + gM_ex_c +                                    \\ (J_{sw} + M_eD^2_T)\ddot{\theta} + B_{sw} \dot{\theta} - g(M_eD_T - M_{sw}D_C)\theta &= 0
	\end{aligned}
	\right.
\end{equation}
For which the Laplace trasnform gives, considering initial states of (0,0):
\begin{equation}
	\left\{
	\begin{aligned}
		(M_e s^2 + (B_{eq,c} + B_{emf})s) \cdot X(s)                                & +                         \\
		(- M_e D_T s^2 + g M_e s) \cdot \Theta (s)                                  & =  \alpha \cdot V_{DC}(s) \\
		(-M_e D_T s^2 + g M_e) \cdot X(s)                                           & +                         \\
		( (J_{sw} + M_e D_T)s^2 + B_{sw}s - g(M_eD_T + M_{sw}D_C)) \cdot \Theta (s) & = 0
	\end{aligned}
	\right.
\end{equation}

Matrix form :
\begin{equation}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		M_e s^2 + (B_{eq,c} + B_{emf})s & -M_e D_T s^2 + g M_e s                                  \\
		-M_e D_T s^2 + g M_e            & (J_{sw} + M_e D_T)s^2 + B_{sw}s - g(M_eD_T + M_{sw}D_C)

		X(s) \\
		\Theta(s)
	\end{bmatrix}
	=
	\begin{bmatrix}
		\alpha \\
		0
	\end{bmatrix}
	V_{DC}(s)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}

using Cramer's rule we can define $G(s)$ and $H(s)$ the transfer functions from $V_{DC}(s)$ to respectively $X(s)$ and $\Theta(s)$.

	B & = \frac{\alpha}{J_{sw}}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		0                          \\
		0                          \\
		\frac{J_{sw}}{M_e} + D_T^2 \\
		D_T
	\end{bmatrix}
\end{align*}
Regarding the output equation, only the positions $x_c$ and the angle $\theta$ are observable and the measurements are not dependent on the input voltage, therefore the output and transmission matrices are :
\begin{align*}
	C                 & =
	\begin{bmatrix}
		1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
		0 & 1 & 0 & 0
	\end{bmatrix} &
	D                 & =
	\begin{bmatrix}
		0 \\
		0
	\end{bmatrix}
\end{align*}
\subsection{Pole Placement State-Feedback Control}
\label{sec:pole_placement}

\subsubsection{Design objective: bounded rocking}

The linearised model predicts $\theta \to 0$ asymptotically. The hardware does not get there. Three unmodelled effects build a deadband around $\theta = 0$ that the controller cannot push through:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Coulomb (static) friction at the cart, which needs a minimum motor voltage to overcome;
	\item theta encoder quantisation, $q_\theta = K_{E,SW}/K_{gs} \approx 5\times10^{-4}\,\text{rad}$ ($\approx 0.029^\circ$/count);
	\item gear backlash and DAC resolution, both of which break the linear, smooth control assumption near zero.
\end{itemize}

The closed loop therefore settles into a limit cycle: the cart cannot generate torque smaller than the deadband threshold, so it lets $\theta$ drift, then reacts when the drift exceeds threshold, sending $\theta$ past the equilibrium. The result is a bounded oscillation -- typically of order $\pm 1^\circ$ -- not a fixed point.

This changes what pole placement is for. The objective is no longer ``drive $\theta$ to zero in $T_s$ seconds.'' It is: \emph{minimise the rocking amplitude}. Each closed-loop pole is chosen with this in mind, not against a settling-time spec.

\subsubsection{Pole-placement framework}

The control law is full state feedback, $V_m = -\mathbf{K}\mathbf{x}$, giving the closed-loop dynamics
\begin{equation}
	\dot{\mathbf{x}} = (A - B\mathbf{K})\,\mathbf{x}, \qquad
	\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_c & \dot{x}_c & \theta & \dot{\theta} \end{bmatrix}^\top.
\end{equation}
The eigenvalues of $A - B\mathbf{K}$ are the closed-loop poles. Because the plant is SISO and the controllability matrix has rank $4$, all four poles can be placed at any desired locations $\{p_1,p_2,p_3,p_4\}$, and $\mathbf{K}$ is then uniquely determined (computed numerically by \texttt{place}).

\paragraph{Complex pole pair parametrisation.} A pair of complex-conjugate poles is conventionally written
\begin{equation}
	p_{1,2} = -\sigma \pm j\,\omega_d
	\qquad\text{with}\qquad
	\sigma = \zeta\,\omega_n, \quad
	\omega_d = \omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}.
	\label{eq:pole_pair}
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
	\item $\omega_n$ -- natural frequency, the distance of the pole from the origin in the s-plane.
	\item $\zeta$ -- damping ratio, $\cos$ of the angle the pole makes with the negative real axis. $\zeta = 1$ is a real double pole; $\zeta = 0$ is a pure undamped oscillation on the $j\omega$ axis.
	\item $\sigma$ -- decay rate (real-part magnitude). The envelope of the second-order impulse response is $e^{-\sigma t}$, so $1/\sigma$ is the time constant of the response.
	\item $\omega_d$ -- damped natural frequency, the actual oscillation frequency seen in the response.
\end{itemize}
Throughout this section, subscript ``$th$'' denotes the dominant pair that governs the $\theta$ loop: $\sigma_{th}, \zeta_{th}, \omega_{n,th}, \omega_{d,th}$.

\paragraph{Pole roles.} For this 4-state plant we use:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $p_{1,2}$: a complex pair governing the $\theta$ dynamics (the inverted-pendulum-like mode);
	\item $p_3$: a real pole damping the cart velocity;
	\item $p_4$: a real, slow pole governing cart-position regulation.
\end{itemize}
Each pole has its own upper and lower bound, derived in the next subsection.

\subsubsection{Open-loop poles}

For the current configuration ($M_c = 0.75\,\text{kg}$, $B_{eq}$ from frequency-sweep tuning):
\begin{equation}
	\lambda_{OL} \;=\; \{+2.24, \;-2.65, \;-0.45, \;-37.0\}\;\text{rad/s}
	\quad\text{(approximate)}
\end{equation}
The pole at $p_{OL} \approx +2.24$ rad/s is the unstable mode -- the seesaw fall rate. Its time constant is $\tau \approx 0.45$ s. Controllability rank is $4/4$, so we have full pole-placement authority.

\subsubsection{Designing the four closed-loop poles}

Two physical limits anchor the design:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $V_{sat} = 6$ V (motor voltage saturation);
	\item $T_c/2 = 0.407$ m (cart rail half-length).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{$p_{1,2}$: complex pair, $\theta$ dynamics.} Parametrised by $(\sigma_{th}, \zeta_{th})$ as in \eqref{eq:pole_pair}.

\textit{Lower bound on $\sigma_{th}$:} the closed loop must outrun divergence. $\sigma_{th} > p_{OL}$ is the bare minimum; we pick $\sigma_{th} \geq 2\,p_{OL} \approx 4.5$ for bandwidth margin against modelling error.

\textit{Upper bound on $\sigma_{th}$:} the gain $K_\theta$ scales roughly as $\sigma_{th}^2$ for this plant. The per-count voltage step from $\theta$ encoder noise is
\begin{equation}
	V_{chatter} = K_\theta \cdot q_\theta.
\end{equation}
This voltage is what the limit cycle feeds on. We demand $V_{chatter} \lesssim 0.1$ V (about $2\%$ of $V_{sat}$); empirically $\sigma_{th} \in [7,9]$ satisfies this. The script prints $V_{chatter}$ so this can be verified after each design iteration.

\textit{Damping $\zeta_{th}$:} during a transient, theta-loop overshoot \emph{is} a rock -- the cart correction sends $\theta$ past zero to the opposite side once before settling. The percent overshoot of a second-order step is
\begin{equation}
	M_p = e^{-\pi\zeta/\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}}\times 100\%
\end{equation}
giving roughly $5\%$ at $\zeta = 0.7$, $1.5\%$ at $\zeta = 0.8$, and $0\%$ at $\zeta = 1$ (but with the slowest decay). $\zeta_{th} = 0.8$ is the chosen compromise.

\textit{Choice:} $\sigma_{th} = 7.0$, $\zeta_{th} = 0.8 \Rightarrow \omega_{n,th} = 8.75$ rad/s, $\omega_{d,th} = 5.25$ rad/s.
\begin{equation}
	p_{1,2} = -7.0 \pm j5.25
\end{equation}

\paragraph{$p_3$: real pole, cart-velocity damping.} Once the cart accelerates to fight a $\theta$ error, $p_3$ governs how fast the cart velocity itself decays.

\textit{Lower bound:} $|p_3| > \sigma_{th}$. If the cart-velocity mode is slower than the $\theta$ mode, the cart overshoots its correction and drives $\theta$ back the other way -- widening the rocking band.

\textit{Upper bound:} $K_{\dot{x}}$ grows with $|p_3|$. Cart velocity is obtained from the encoder by numerical differentiation, so its quantum is large: $q_{\dot{x}} \sim K_{ec}/T_s \approx 0.023$ m/s per count at $T_s = 1$ ms. Excessive $|p_3|$ injects this noise into the voltage command.

\textit{Choice:} $p_3 = -1.5\,\sigma_{th} = -10.5$ rad/s.

\paragraph{$p_4$: real pole, cart-position regulation (slowest).} Sets the time constant for the cart returning to centre after being displaced, $\tau \approx 1/|p_4|$.

\textit{Lower bound:} under bias the cart settles offset (Section~\ref{sssec:bias}). A 100 g offset gives tens of cm of cart drift; with $T_c/2 = 40.7$ cm and a target return time of $\sim 3$ s, we need $|p_4| \gtrsim 0.3$ rad/s.

\textit{Upper bound:} faster than $\sim 3$ rad/s puts cart-position regulation on the same timescale as the $\theta$ loop. The cart then actively chases position errors that should be left to relax slowly, fighting the $\theta$ controller and widening the limit cycle.

\textit{Choice:} $p_4 = -1.0$ rad/s (1 s time constant).

\paragraph{Final pole set.}
\begin{equation}
	p_{\mathrm{des}} \;=\; \{\,-7.0 + j5.25,\; -7.0 - j5.25,\; -10.5,\; -1.0\,\}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Bias torque -- shifts the rocking centre}
\label{sssec:bias}

A 100 g mass placed off-centre on the seesaw produces a constant gravitational torque
\begin{equation}
	\tau_{bias} = m\,g\,D_T \approx 0.123\;\text{N}\cdot\text{m}.
\end{equation}
Without integral action, the closed loop converges to a non-zero steady-state $\theta_{ss}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:rep_final}). On hardware this means the limit cycle is \emph{centred} on $\theta_{ss}$ rather than on zero -- the rocking band is shifted, not widened. This is the natural behaviour of a regulator with no model of the disturbance.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{../figures/repeated_disturbance.png}
	\caption{Bias-torque response without integral action. The cart converges, but to a non-zero $\theta_{ss}$ -- on hardware this offset becomes the centre of the rocking band.}
	\label{fig:rep_final}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Integral action -- recenters the rocking band}

To remove the bias-induced offset, the state is augmented with an integrator on $\theta$:
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{x}_a = \begin{bmatrix} x_c & \dot{x}_c & \theta & \dot{\theta} & \xi \end{bmatrix}^\top,
	\qquad \dot{\xi} = \theta,
	\qquad V_m = -\mathbf{K}_a\,\mathbf{x}_a.
\end{equation}
The four pole-placement poles are retained and one integrator pole is added at $p_{int} = -0.3$ rad/s, slower than $p_4$ so it does not dominate the cart dynamics:
\begin{equation}
	p_{\mathrm{aug}} = \{\,-7.0 \pm j5.25,\; -10.5,\; -1.0,\; -0.3\,\}
\end{equation}

What integral action does and does not do:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Does:} drive $\overline{\theta}\to 0$ under a constant disturbance, so the rocking band is symmetric about zero.
	\item \textbf{Does not:} reduce the rocking amplitude. The deadband effects that produce the limit cycle are independent of the steady-state offset; the integrator only relocates the centre.
\end{itemize}

	\caption{Bias-torque response with integral action. The steady-state offset is removed; on hardware the rocking band is now centred on zero, with amplitude similar to the no-integral case.}
	\label{fig:bias_integral}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Luenberger State Observer}
\label{sssec:observer}

The pole-placement law assumes the full state $\mathbf{x} = [x_c,\ \dot{x}_c,\ \theta,\ \dot{\theta}]^\top$ is available, but only $x_c$ and $\theta$ are encoded; the velocities are not directly measured. The naive workaround is to differentiate each encoder and follow it with a first-order low-pass filter. That scheme has no plant model -- it trades phase lag for noise attenuation blindly, and the lag is paid into the same loop that has to outrun $p_{OL}$. A model-based estimator avoids that trade-off.

\paragraph{Theory (minimal).}
With the measurement matrix
\begin{equation}
	C_{\mathrm{meas}} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix},
	\qquad y = C_{\mathrm{meas}}\mathbf{x},
\end{equation}
the Luenberger observer
\begin{equation}
	\dot{\hat{\mathbf{x}}} = A\hat{\mathbf{x}} + B u + L\,(y - C_{\mathrm{meas}}\hat{\mathbf{x}})
	\label{eq:luenberger}
\end{equation}
produces an estimate $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ whose error $e = \mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}$ obeys
\begin{equation}
	\dot{e} = (A - L\,C_{\mathrm{meas}})\,e.
	\label{eq:err_dyn}
\end{equation}
If $(A, C_{\mathrm{meas}})$ is observable, the eigenvalues of $A - LC_{\mathrm{meas}}$ can be assigned arbitrarily by choice of $L$. Substituting $u = -\mathbf{K}\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ into the plant and writing the combined system in $(\mathbf{x}, e)$ coordinates gives the block-triangular form
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\!\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x} \\ e \end{bmatrix}
	= \begin{bmatrix} A - B\mathbf{K} & B\mathbf{K} \\ 0 & A - LC_{\mathrm{meas}} \end{bmatrix}\!\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x} \\ e \end{bmatrix}.
	\label{eq:separation}
\end{equation}
Block-triangularity gives the \emph{separation principle}: $\operatorname{eig}(\cdot) = \operatorname{eig}(A - B\mathbf{K}) \cup \operatorname{eig}(A - LC_{\mathrm{meas}})$. Controller and observer are designed independently; the gain $\mathbf{K}$ from \S\ref{sec:pole_placement} is reused unchanged.

The dual identity
\begin{equation}
	\operatorname{eig}(A - LC_{\mathrm{meas}}) = \operatorname{eig}\!\big((A^\top - C_{\mathrm{meas}}^\top L^\top)^\top\big)
\end{equation}
turns the observer-gain problem into a pole-placement problem on the pair $(A^\top, C_{\mathrm{meas}}^\top)$, solved with \texttt{place} just as for $\mathbf{K}$.

\paragraph{Tuning plan.}
Each observer pole is chosen against an explicit objective with bounds, in the same style as the controller poles. Let $\sigma_{\mathrm{obs}} = \min_i |\Re(p_{\mathrm{obs},i})|$ denote the slowest observer mode.

\textit{Lower bound (separation).} The estimate must catch up before the controller acts on it; otherwise feedback is built on stale state and the loop behaves like a delayed full-state system. The standard rule
\begin{equation}
	|\Re(p_{\mathrm{obs}})| \;\geq\; (3{-}5)\,|\Re(p_{\mathrm{ctrl}})|
\end{equation}
guarantees a clean time-scale separation.

\textit{Upper bound (noise).} Each encoder count $\delta y_i$ propagates to the input as $\delta V_m = (\mathbf{K} L) \delta y_i$. With encoder quanta
\begin{equation}
	q_{x_c} = K_{ec} \approx 2.275\times 10^{-5}\,\text{m},
	\qquad q_\theta = K_{E,SW}/K_{gs} \approx 5\times 10^{-4}\,\text{rad},
\end{equation}
the per-count voltage chatter must stay well under $V_{sat}$; we demand $|(\mathbf{K} L)_i|\,q_i \lesssim 0.1$ V on each channel. Increasing the observer-pole multiplier $k_{\mathrm{obs}}$ inflates $L$ and therefore this product.

\textit{Damping.} Estimation overshoot is internal and does not directly distort the loop, but high damping shortens error decay. Mirror the controller-pair shape ($\zeta = 0.8$) so the observer behaves predictably in transients.

\textit{Choice.} We sweep $k_{\mathrm{obs}}$ across the textbook band and read the noise gain at each value. The result decides where to land:

\end{equation}
This sits below the textbook lower bound, but the practical concern that motivates the lower bound (estimate stale relative to controller bandwidth) is satisfied: the dominant theta-loop modes shift from $-7.0 \pm j5.25$ for the controller to $-17.5 \pm j13.1$ for the observer, $2.5\times$ faster on the modes that matter. The slowest observer pole at $-2.5$ rad/s regulates the estimation error of cart \emph{position} -- a state directly measured by the encoder -- so leaving it slow has no functional cost: the observer's $\hat{x}_c$ tracks $x_{c,\mathrm{meas}}$ via the $L_{1,1}$ feedthrough almost instantaneously regardless of where that eigenvalue sits.

\paragraph{Verification.}
The design script \texttt{luenberger\_observer\_design.m} runs all six checks. Numerical results for the chosen $k_{\mathrm{obs}} = 2.5$:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Observability.} $\operatorname{rank}(\mathcal{O}) = 4$, $\operatorname{cond}(\mathcal{O}) = 3.7\times 10^{2}$ -- well-conditioned. Both states behind each encoder (velocity behind position, $\dot\theta$ behind $\theta$) are recoverable.
	\item \textbf{Gain.}
	\begin{equation}
		L = \begin{bmatrix} 23.88 & 7.41 \\ 17.58 & 70.75 \\ -15.20 & 21.09 \\ -46.01 & 74.84 \end{bmatrix}.
	\end{equation}
	The dominant entries are in the $\theta$ column, second and fourth rows -- $\theta$-measurement is the dominant correction term for both velocity estimates, as the dynamic coupling predicts.
	\item \textbf{Separation principle.} Eigenvalue mismatch between the combined $8\times 8$ matrix and the union of controller/observer spectra: $1.1\times 10^{-14}$, i.e. machine precision. The construction is algebraically correct.
	\item \textbf{IC error decay.} The hardware is hand-placed near horizontal, typically within $1{-}2^\circ$ of zero -- it will rock in a limit cycle anyway, so a perfect alignment is neither possible nor required. We use $\theta(0) = 2^\circ$ as the realistic startup excursion (the $4.5^\circ$ used in the controller IC test is an extreme bump-recovery check, not a representative start condition). With $\hat{\mathbf{x}}(0)=0$ and $\mathbf{x}(0) = [0,0,2^\circ,0]^\top$, all four error components fall inside their encoder-quantum tolerance by $t = 1.81$ s, consistent with the slowest observer pole at $-2.5$ rad/s.
	\item \textbf{Loop in the loop.} The combined system response is plotted in Fig.~\ref{fig:obs_ic}.
	\item \textbf{Noise injection.}
	\begin{equation}
		|\mathbf{K} L|_1\,q_{x_c} = 0.21\;\text{V/count}, \qquad
		|\mathbf{K} L|_2\,q_\theta = 1.86\;\text{V/count}.
	\end{equation}
	The $\theta$-channel gain is $32\times$ the controller-only $V_{chatter} = 0.059$ V/count.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Engineering implications of the noise figure.}
The $1.86$ V/count number deserves explicit comment because it looks alarming. It is the \emph{static} (DC) gain from a single-count perturbation on $\theta$ to $V_m$; it would only be reached if a 1-count error persisted across the full observer time constant. In practice:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Encoder quantization is high-frequency dither, sampled at $1$ kHz; the observer bandwidth ($\approx 17.5$ rad/s $\approx 2.8$ Hz dominant pair, $\approx 26$ rad/s real pole) is three orders of magnitude below the sampling rate, so most of the quantization energy is filtered out by $A - LC$ before reaching $\mathbf{K}\hat{\mathbf{x}}$.
	\item The figure to compare against on hardware is the steady-state limit-cycle voltage RMS, not the per-count step. Expect chatter inflation by a factor of $\sim 5{-}10\times$ over the controller-only baseline, not $32\times$.
	\item The trade is honest: the differentiator + LPF currently in use has its own DC gain explosion (the LPF at $\omega_f = 100$ rad/s passes a per-count $\dot\theta$ pulse at $K_{\dot\theta} \cdot \omega_f \cdot q_\theta$, which for $K_{\dot\theta} \sim 1$ V$\cdot$s/rad already gives $\sim 0.05$ V at high frequency but with much worse phase margin). The observer gives less phase loss for the same noise budget.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Note on the augmented controller.}
With integral action the controller state is $\mathbf{x}_a = [\mathbf{x}; \xi]$ where $\dot{\xi} = \theta$. Since $\theta$ is measured directly, $\xi$ is integrated from the encoder reading, not from $\hat{\theta}$. Only the four plant states are estimated; the observer carries over to the augmented controller without modification.

\subsubsection{Implementation and Hardware Results}

On hardware, only $x_c$ and $\theta$ are measured by encoders. The Luenberger observer of \S\ref{sssec:observer} reconstructs $\dot{x}_c$ and $\dot{\theta}$ from the encoder pair using the linearised plant model, replacing the differentiator + low-pass filter scheme.
